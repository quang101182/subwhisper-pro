2. VEED.io

Free / $24/mo

VEED.io is a popular browser-based video editor that includes auto-subtitling as one of its core features. The interface is polished and beginner-friendly — you upload a video, click "Auto Subtitle," and get results within minutes. VEED supports 50+ languages and lets you customize subtitle styling directly in the editor.

The main drawback is the free tier. You're limited to 2 GB uploads, 10 minutes of subtitles per month, and every export gets a VEED watermark burned into your video. Removing the watermark and unlocking unlimited minutes requires the Business plan at $24/month, which is steep for creators just starting out.

Accuracy was solid at around 93% in our tests, though it struggled more with non-English audio compared to Whisper-based tools. The translation features are basic — single-pass only — and sometimes produce awkward phrasing.

Pros

  • Beautiful, intuitive interface
  • Built-in video editor
  • Custom subtitle styling
  • No software to install

Cons

  • Watermark on free tier
  • $24/mo is pricey
  • 10 min/month free limit
  • Average non-English accuracy
Best for: Users who want an all-in-one browser video editor and don't mind paying $24/month. Less ideal if subtitling is your primary need.

3. Kapwing

Free / $24/mo

Kapwing is another browser-based video editor with solid auto-captioning capabilities. Its subtitle tool supports 70+ languages and offers a clean editing interface where you can review and fix each caption line. Kapwing's collaboration features also make it useful for teams working on video projects together.

Like VEED, the free tier has significant limitations: exports include a Kapwing watermark, you're limited to 720p exports, and projects over 4 minutes require a paid plan. The Pro plan costs $24/month — the same as VEED — which makes both tools a hard sell when tools like SubWhisper offer unlimited free transcription.

Accuracy was comparable to VEED at around 93%. Kapwing does offer a useful "smart cut" feature that can remove silence from videos based on the subtitle timing, which is a nice productivity bonus for short-form content creators.

4. CapCut

Free

CapCut, owned by ByteDance (the parent company of TikTok), offers free auto-captioning as part of its video editor. It's especially popular among short-form content creators because of its tight integration with TikTok workflows. The auto-caption feature is accessible on both desktop and mobile, which is convenient for creators who edit on the go.

The accuracy, however, lags behind Whisper-based tools. In our tests, CapCut scored around 90% accuracy for English and noticeably worse for other languages. It only supports about 20 languages for auto-captioning, which is limiting for international creators. Subtitle export options are also restricted — you can get SRT files, but only through a somewhat clunky workflow.

CapCut's strength is its trendy subtitle templates and animations, which are perfect for TikTok and Instagram Reels. If accuracy and language support aren't your top priorities, CapCut is a genuinely free option with no watermarks or hidden costs.

5. Happy Scribe

It's one of the more mature options in this space, offering both automatic AI transcription and human-assisted transcription at higher price points. The platform supports 60+ languages and offers export to a wide range of formats including SRT, VTT, TXT, and STL.

There's no truly free tier — Happy Scribe offers a limited trial, then pricing starts at $9/month for basic auto-transcription. The accuracy is competitive at around 94%, and the subtitle editor is well-designed with features like speaker identification and timestamps you can adjust frame-by-frame.

For professional use, Happy Scribe offers human proofreading services starting at $1.70/minute, which guarantees 99%+ accuracy. This hybrid approach (AI + human review) is valuable for broadcast or corporate video where errors are unacceptable.

6. Canva

Free / $13/mo

Canva added auto-subtitling to its video editor in late 2024, and while it's a welcome feature for existing Canva users, it's fairly basic compared to dedicated tools. The subtitle feature is available on both free and Pro plans, but free users are limited to a small number of transcription minutes per month.

Language support is minimal at only 18 languages, and accuracy was the second-lowest in our tests at around 88%. The biggest limitation is that Canva only offers burned-in subtitles — there's no way to export an SRT or VTT file. This means you can't upload your subtitles to YouTube or use them with other video platforms. Your subtitles are permanently baked into the video.

That said, if you already use Canva for your design workflow and just need basic English captions for social media posts, it's a convenient option that doesn't require switching to another tool. The subtitle styling options are decent, with templates that match Canva's design-forward philosophy.

7. YouTube Auto-Captions

Free

YouTube's built-in automatic captioning system has been around for over a decade, and while it has improved over the years, it still falls behind dedicated subtitle generators in both accuracy and features. YouTube auto-generates captions for uploaded videos in about 13 languages, and you can download the generated SRT file from YouTube Studio.

The biggest issue is accuracy. YouTube's auto-captions scored around 85% in our tests — the lowest of any tool on this list. Punctuation is often missing or incorrect, speaker changes aren't detected, and technical terms or proper nouns are frequently garbled. You'll spend significant time cleaning up the output.

The one clear advantage is that it's completely free with no limitations for videos you upload to YouTube. If you're already uploading to YouTube anyway and just need a rough draft to edit, it can save time compared to typing subtitles from scratch. How We Tested These Subtitle Generators

To make this comparison as fair as possible, we used the same source material for every tool: a 10-minute video featuring clear speech in English, a 5-minute French podcast clip with moderate background music, and a 3-minute Japanese news segment. Here's what we measured:

  • Accuracy: We compared AI-generated subtitles against a human-verified reference transcript, measuring word error rate (WER) for each tool.
  • Timing precision: How closely subtitle timestamps matched actual speech boundaries. Off-by-more-than-500ms was flagged as an error.
  • Language support: Verified language counts against each tool's claims by testing at least 5 non-English languages.
  • Export flexibility: Tested all available export formats and verified they imported correctly into Premiere Pro, DaVinci Resolve, and YouTube Studio.
  • Real cost: Calculated the actual monthly cost to subtitle 60 minutes of video per month (a typical creator workload).

All tests were conducted in March 2026 using the latest available versions of each tool.

What to Look for in a Free Subtitle Generator

Not all auto subtitle generators are created equal. Here are the key factors to consider when choosing one:

Accuracy Matters More Than Speed

A tool that transcribes your video in 30 seconds but gets 15% of words wrong will cost you more time fixing errors than a slightly slower tool with 96% accuracy. Whisper-based tools (like SubWhisper) consistently outperform older speech-to-text engines, especially for non-English content.

Check Export Format Support

If you need subtitles for YouTube, make sure the tool exports SRT files. For web embedding, you'll want VTT. For advanced styling in video editors, ASS/SSA format is essential. Avoid tools that only offer burned-in subtitles unless that's specifically what you need.

Watch Out for "Free" Limitations

Many tools advertise as "free" but add watermarks, limit export quality, or cap your monthly minutes at impractical levels. A genuine free subtitle generator should let you transcribe without watermarks — SubWhisper and CapCut are the only two tools on this list that truly deliver on that promise.

Consider Privacy

Cloud-based tools upload your video to their servers for processing. If you're working with confidential content (corporate training, legal depositions, unreleased media), a local tool like SubWhisper that processes everything on your machine is significantly more secure.
